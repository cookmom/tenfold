The mathematics of Islamic geometric patterns

For over a thousand years, artists across the Islamic world have created geometric patterns of extraordinary complexity using only two tools: a compass and a straightedge. The patterns that emerge from these instruments — stars, rosettes, interlacing bands — decorate mosques, palaces, and manuscripts from Morocco to Malaysia.

This guide explores their mathematical foundations through interactive demonstrations. Drag the sliders. Watch the constructions. The patterns are not drawn — they emerge.

1 · The Circle

Every Islamic geometric pattern begins with a single circle. In Islamic cosmology, the circle represents tawhid — the unity of God. From this one form, all diversity in creation unfolds.

"All Islamic geometric patterns originate from the harmonious subdivisions of circles. The circle is a symbol of a religion that emphasizes One God and the role of Mecca, which is the center of Islam toward which all Muslims pray." — Abdullahi & Embi, Frontiers of Architectural Research, 2013

The natural division of the circle into equal parts is the ritual starting point. Divide into 4 and you get the cross of cardinal directions. Into 6, the hexagon — basis of the Flower of Life. Into 8, the octagram — the khatam, master tile of Moroccan zellige. Into 10, the shamsa — Mr Oshaghi's "little sun", the seed of Persian patterns.

2 · Three Proportional Systems

Not all Islamic patterns are the same. Three distinct proportional systems emerged across the Islamic world, each rooted in a different irrational number — and each producing a fundamentally different visual character.

√2 — Morocco & Andalusia

The ratio √2 and the silver ratio (1+√2) underlie the 8, 16, and 32-fold rosettes of the western Islamic world. The Alhambra in Granada, the Qarawiyyn mosque in Fez, the madrasas of Marrakech — all built on the square grid and its diagonal.

√3 — Syria & Egypt

The hexagonal grid, based on √3, generates 6 and 12-fold patterns. The Umayyad Mosque in Damascus, the Mosque of Ibn Tulun in Cairo. The Flower of Life — circles packed in hexagonal arrangement — is the construction foundation.

φ — Iran & Central Asia

The golden section φ = (1+√5)/2 ≈ 1.618 produces 5 and 10-fold symmetry. Isfahan's Friday Mosque, the Darb-i Imam shrine. The shamsa (decagram) is the seed. These are the patterns that, in 2007, were shown to be quasicrystalline — anticipating Penrose tilings by five centuries.

"The ratio of edge length to height is 1 to the golden section. The Greeks called such numbers 'unutterables'. Despite this, these values could be determined geometrically with great precision." — Richard Henry, Journal of the Iran Society, 2007
India · Agra Fort
The 4-fold system builds from interlocking squares — static and dynamic — inscribed within a circle. From their intersections, a family of small tangent circles grows. Lines through their centers create the grid from which the Mughal 8-pointed star lattice emerges.

3 · The Construction

For centuries, the compass and straightedge were the only tools used to construct these patterns. The method is universal: begin with a circle, subdivide, place new circles at intersections, connect — and the pattern reveals itself.

"It is important to remember that Ka'ba means cube. The Ka'ba is a cubic form — the earthly square — circumscribed by the tawaf, the circular path of circumambulation. Heaven and earth, circle and square, are united in this form." — Titus Burckhardt, Art of Islam: Language and Meaning

Four traditions. Four construction methods. Each rooted in a different proportional system, each revealing a different face of the divine geometry. Step through any of them below — every circle and line follows exactly the compass-and-ruler procedure documented by Sandy Kurt from mosques across the Islamic world.

The pattern is then developed through intelligent extension of parallels forming a network of lines, at the intersections of which the design emerges." — Richard Henry on Master Oshaghi's workshop, Isfahan

4 · The Tiling

A single rosette is beautiful. But Islamic patterns were never meant to stand alone — they were designed to tile infinitely. The unit cell from the 8-fold construction, repeated across a surface, creates an unbroken field of interlocking stars. This is the "aha moment": one rosette becomes an infinite field.

"Islamic ornament operates at two scales simultaneously — the individual unit, and the infinite extension. The tile defines its own limits while implying no limit at all." — Keith Critchlow, Islamic Patterns: An Analytical and Cosmological Approach

Adjust the grid size to see how the pattern scales. Each repetition is the same unit cell — the khatam rosette — perfectly interlocked with its neighbors.

5 · Self-Similarity

What makes these proportions feel harmonious? The answer lies in self-similarity — the same shapes recurring at different scales, like a fractal. In nature, bones, shells, and the DNA helix all grow by this principle. Islamic patterns encode the same law.

"The smaller to the larger is as the larger to the whole — this is the signature of living forms. It underlies the proportioning of bones in the human body, as well as the structuring of the DNA spiral." — Richard Henry, Pattern, Cognition and Contemplation

In 2007, Peter Lu and Paul Steinhardt demonstrated that the tiles on the Darb-i Imam shrine in Isfahan (1453) exhibit two levels of self-similar quasicrystalline structure — the same mathematical property that Roger Penrose described in 1974, five centuries later.

5 · The Shamsa — 10-Fold Decagonal Pattern

The 10-fold pattern is the library's namesake. In Persian it is called shamsa (شمسه) — "sun." It appears in the Darb-i Imam shrine in Isfahan (1453 CE), one of the most mathematically sophisticated buildings ever constructed.

Unlike all other fold systems, 10-fold geometry is governed entirely by the golden ratio φ = (1+√5)/2 ≈ 1.618. The decagon side length equals R/φ exactly. The {10/3} star's inner decagon sits at radius R/φ. Every intersection, every proportion, every self-similar level is a power of 1/φ.

6 · The Seven-Fold Mystery

7-fold symmetry is the frontier. The regular heptagon cannot be constructed with compass and straightedge — this was proven impossible (7 is not a Fermat prime, and cannot be expressed as a product of distinct Fermat primes with a power of 2). Yet Islamic craftsmen created 7-fold patterns in the 12th century CE using approximate methods with < 0.5° cumulative error.

The earliest known example: the Ghaznavid minaret of Mas'ud III in Ghazna, Afghanistan, 1099–1115 CE. Jay Bonner and Marc Pelletier documented the systematic 7/14 construction in their 2012 Bridges paper, revealing how heptagonal sub-grids generate these rare patterns.

7 · Pattern Metamorphosis
